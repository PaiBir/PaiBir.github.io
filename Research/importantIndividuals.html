  • Albertus Magnus

    Writer of the De Mineralibus

    Disagreed heavilly with the "Callisthenes" (Khalid) view of all metals being various states of completion of gold, due to their stability.

    Disagrees with Sciant Artifices, by interpreting species as "Specific Form"

    Excerpt from the De Mineralibus[1]

    Alchemy proceeds through this method, namely by corrupting one <metal> through a removal <of it> from its own species, and by inducing the species of another with the aid of those <natures> that are in matter...for in these <sulfur and mercury> the species of every metal are induced...

    A breakdown:

    According to Albertus, a metal can be transmuted not through direct transmutation, but by taking it the long way around. Albertus suggests that by removing its defining characteristics, its species can be removed. And then by introducing another set of characteristics from another species, let nature turn that formless material into another species.

    As Newman points out, by replacing species with "specific form", Albertus sidesteps Sciant Artifeces, and pulls on established literature on physical corruption of a form, with a subsequent transformation into another form. This is, in and of itself, a corruption and subsequent transformation of the "species" used in De Congelatione, which Avicenna originally used as a "logical entity", similar the use of genus by logicians.[1]

    However, the important detail which distinguishes the Albertian view of Alchemy from the Paulian lies in methodology. Albertian alchemy mimics nature, with a rather mystical outlook on the world (fire mimicing stars, and alchemical instruments mimicing the caverns where metals grow). Paulian alchemy, as we will see later, is not quite as whimisical.
    As such, Albertian alchemy is likely better for my setting, as this produces, in my estimation, richer worldbuilding potential.

  • Roger Bacon

    Writer of the Opus Minus and Opus Tertium. An excitable friar who was prone to hyperbole.

    Excerpt from the Opus Tertium[1]

    But there is another science which is about the generation of things from the elements, and about all inanimate things, for example the elements, simple and compounded humors, common stones, gems, and types of marble, gold, and other metals, sulfurs, salts, and inks, azures, minium, and other colors, oils and burning pitches, and countless other things of which we have nothing in the books of Aristotle: nor do natural philosophers know of these things, nor the whole Latin crowd. And since this science is ignored by the mass of students, it is necessary that they be ignroant of all natural things that follow therefrom, for example the generation of animated things, such as vegetables, animals, and men, for prior things having been ignored, it is necessary that posterior things be ignored... Whence, due to ignorance of this science, common natural consequently, practical medicine, not only because natural philosophy and theoretical medicine are necessary for its practice, but because all simple medicines from inanimate things are recieved from this science... Nor can the names of medicins or their significations be known without this science. And this science is called "theoretical alchemy," which theorizes about all inanimate things and about the generation of things from the elements. There is in adition an operative and practical alchemy, which teaches how to make noble metals, colors, and many other things - better and more plentifully by art than they are produced by nature. And a science of this sort is greater than all the preceding, because it produces greater utility. Not only can it provide the expenditures and countless other <needs> of the republic, but it teaches how to discover such things as can greatly prolong human life, which cannot be arrived at by nature.

  • Paulus of Taranto

    Alternatives to Taranto:

    • Tarento

    • Toronto

    Wrote the Summa Perfectionis and Theorica et practica

    Paulian alchemy splits the Art into two genera. The first of these is the basal elements, while the second is accidental properties. Paulian alchemy believes the second genus to be what Aristotle refers to as the art which cannot surpass nature. By essence of acting on purely sensory properties, is "not transmutative of substance". The first genus, however, can influence nature to enact change.

    Excerpts from the Theorica et practica[1]

    Art exists solely in the role of mover and director, of supporter and uler, but not in the role of maker. Nature herself is then in the role of mover, maker, or causer...

    ...such men make essential things, not merely accidental ones. And since nature actually does the making in all things, while art only administers, joins together, and rules, the effect must be attributed to nature than to art, or to nature beneath <the rule of> art.

    We do not consider the opinion of Aristotle which he writes at the end of the Meteors, "The alchemists should know that species cannot be transmuted" to be true unless it be understood in the foresaid way <so that transmutation can occur> through purely artificial agents.

    ...anything short of the animated and the soul itself can be made naturally from anything with regard to elementary form ... such as bodies composed of the four elements, as for example stone and metals.

    Breakdown:

    The first genera of Paulian alchemy directs nature, but nature enacts the change. It is like prepping the pieces of an IKEA flatpack and then writing the instruction manual, before handing it off to nature to actually make. The second genera can only inflict visible changes, but cannot affect the deeper material.

    Paul also does not appear to have been aware that Sciant Artifices was not an Aristotelian idea. However, he does appear to have sidestepped it in a way similar to Albertus, and reminicent of Aristotle's idea of an art which can surpass nature, and one that can't. Specifically, the first genera is similar to Albertus' idea of transmution, although with less mysticism, and the second genera does not violate Avicenna's dispersion of Alchemy. Both genera fufill Aristotle's view on nature.

  • Thomas Aquinas

    Staunch supporter of Sciant Artifices. Compared Demonology and Alchemy. However, many of his works were completed posthumously, so his actual view of alchemy is not plainly apparent. And if Sciant Artifices holds up for demons, surely it does for alchemists.

    Art by its own power cannot confer a substantial form, but it can do this by means of a natural agent, as is clear in the following, that the form of fire is produced in logs through art. There are some substantial forms, however, which art cannot produce by any means, since it cannot find the proper active and passive subjects. Even in these art can produce a similtude, as when alchemists produce something similar to gold as to exterior accidents. But it is still not true gold, since the substantial form of gold is not [induced] by the heat of fire - which alchemists use - but by the heat of the sun in a determinate place where the mineral power flourishes. Hence such [alchemical] gold does not operate according to the species [of real gold], and the same is true for the other things that they [alchemists] make.

    Clearly, Thomas is voicing his belief in Sciant Artifices, or more importantly, his belief on how it operates, and how, from it, to explain what alchemists do do. It is an interesting idea, suggesting that we are limited by our knowledge.

Greek

  • Aristotle

    Aristotle is arguably the backbone of 13th century Alchemy. Aristotelianism and Aristotle's various books form the foundation for Natural Philosophy.

    Aristotle's own take on the role of art is described as such:

    One sort of art perfects that which nature cannot complete, wile another sort imitates nature.

    My own take on this line is that there are various Arts. Some which can surpass nature, and others which cannot. This is a similar opinion to that of Paul of Taranto, with his two genera of the Art.

  • Hermes Trismegistus

    Mercurius ter Maximus is a likely fictional, or at the very least, highly exaggerated, alchemist, who is the origin of Hermeticism. Hermes Trismegistus was thought to be a combination of Hermes and Thoth. Similar to Geber, Hermes Trismegistus was a common pseudepigraph.

    Hermes Trismegistus is attributed to many books, including a Liber Hermetis which was unlabeled and included into the Interrogations and Responses of the Liber de compositione alchemiae by a scribe. The Magarita pretiosa cites this Liber Hermetis believing it to from Morienus.

  • Ibn Sina

    Avicenna, in the text inserted into Meteors, supports mercury theory, however actively denounces transmutation. Because this idea was connected to Aristotle, and the weight his texts had in the alchemical community, finding Avicenna's ideas supposedly triggered a wave of "Neoplatonizing Aristotelianism"[1] (Deductive and Inductive reasoning to explain the world, with a mix of more mystical elements) within European Alchemists.

    Avicennian species are, as described by Newman, abstract categories beyond mortal comprehension. Transmutation of such species would constitute humans having the ability to overcome God's will, and reform what he made. This makes Avicennian species more concrete than Albertian species. Depending on which species you prefer, the malleability of the natural world is either quite low, or extremely high.

    The basis of "Sciant Artifices"

    • Artificial products are intrinsically both different and inferior from their Natural counterparts. The Art can mimic, but not surpass Nature.

    • Humans cannot know the true characteristics which seperate one species of metal from another, as these are buried beyond the reach of human senses.

    • "Accidental" properties (physical characteristics which are not key to species) can be changed by humans with ease.

    • Metals are distinguished by the ratio of their components. As such, transmutation is only possible by transmuting the base components of the metal, which cn only be done if the metal is broken down to these components. Combining metals does not trigger this breakdown, but combines the "Accidental" properties

  • Jabir ibn Hayyan

    Geber is a (potentially non-existent) Islamic alchemist whose name has been used *extensively* by many alchemists as a pseudepigraph. There are many works written solely in Latin, which are known to not belong to Jabir ibn Hayyan. However, there is also evidence that suggests that even the Arabic works attributed to Jabir ibn Hayyan may haven been produced by Arabic students using the name for authority.
